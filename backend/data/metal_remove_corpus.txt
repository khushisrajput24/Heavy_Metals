LEAD

Effective removal of lead from water is critical for ensuring environmental safety, public health, and ecosystem preservation. Based on the reference materials and additional insights, the following methods represent viable strategies for lead removal. Each method has specific advantages and limitations based on factors such as lead concentration, water composition, operational costs, and scalability.

---

### **1. Adsorption-Based Techniques**
Adsorption is one of the most effective and widely used methodologies for lead removal, particularly due to its cost-efficiency and adaptability to diverse water chemistries.

- **[Activated Carbon](@brm/pedia/kw:268402) and Biochar**: Activated carbon prepared from agricultural or industrial waste demonstrates excellent lead adsorption capacities due to its high surface area and porosity. Biochar made from biomass materials (such as coconut shells or agricultural residues) provides an eco-friendly alternative with comparable adsorption efficiency (Zhang et al., 2024)(Azimi et al., 2017).
  
- **Metal Oxides and Functionalized Nanomaterials**:
  - Iron oxides, zinc oxide, and hybrid materials (e.g., ZnO-GO-NiO nanocomposites) exhibit strong lead adsorption through chemical and electrostatic interactions with lead ions. Such materials are particularly beneficial for their high removal capacities and potential reusability (Hajdu et al., 2012)(Maqbool et al., 2023).
  - Alumina nanofibers functionalized with amines show a high affinity for lead ions, achieving close to 80% removal under optimal conditions, depending on pH and ionic strength (Houston et al., 2023).

- **Natural Adsorbents**: Plant residues, such as orange and pomegranate peels, maize cobs, eggshells, and Moringa oleifera, offer sustainable solutions. These materials contain functional groups (e.g., hydroxyl, carboxyl, amino) that effectively bind lead at the molecular level (shree, 2024)(Ali, 2021)(Muthusamy & Murugan, 2016).

- **Chitosan-Based Systems**: Chitosan polymers (natural polysaccharides derived from crustaceans) with Janus morphology have been functionalized to significantly improve lead adsorption. The addition of sodium borohydride (NaBH4) further enhances adsorption using combined chemical reduction and ion exchange (Khoee et al., 2024).

---

### **2. Ion Exchange**
Ion exchange techniques exploit resins capable of replacing undesirable lead ions in water with innocuous ions (e.g., sodium or hydrogen).

- **Synthetic Resins**: Engineered ion-exchange resins effectively remove lead even under high ionic strength conditions (e.g., saltwater). They can be regenerated multiple times, making them a cost-effective choice for certain industrial applications (Kamal & Rafey, 2021).
- **Zeolitic Materials**: Zeolitic imidazolate frameworks (ZIFs) and natural zeolites demonstrate remarkable selectivity for lead(II) ions in water. These materials provide a tunable and highly efficient solution to lead contamination (Ahmad et al., 2022).

---

### **3. Membrane Filtration**
Membrane technologies are increasingly utilized due to their capability to separate ions, molecules, and other contaminants with precision.

- **Reverse Osmosis (RO)**: RO membranes effectively reject heavy metal ions, including lead, via size exclusion. However, high-pressure systems increase operational costs, making this method more expensive for large-scale use (Hajdu et al., 2012).
  
- **Electrodialysis and Shock Electrodialysis**:
  - Electrodialysis uses ion-exchange membranes to separate lead ions through a voltage gradient.
  - In shock electrodialysis, the localized electric field selectively removes lead while allowing other ions to pass, achieving over 95% lead removal even in the presence of competing ions (Tian et al., 2021)(Gardon et al., 2020).

- **Innovative Membrane Materials**:
  - PVA/PAA crosslinked membranes provide a sustainable, electricity-free solution for lead removal, especially under emergency conditions (Zhang et al., 2021).
  - Cellulose acetate membranes from water hyacinth and advanced materials like ZnO-PVA or ZnO-GO-NiO membranes achieve high selectivity and efficiency in lead removal (Amalia et al., 2023)(Rasheed et al., 2023)(Maqbool et al., 2023).

---

### **4. Chemical Precipitation**
Chemical precipitation is a traditional method frequently used for lead removal in wastewater treatment plants.

- **Use of Chemicals**: Chemicals such as lime (CaO), sodium hydroxide (NaOH), or sodium carbonate (Na2CO3) facilitate the precipitation of lead in the form of insoluble compounds, such as lead hydroxide (Pb(OH)2) or lead carbonate (PbCO3) (Vu et al., 2019)(Azimi et al., 2017).
- **Limitations**: While effective, this method generates solid waste requiring further treatment and is less efficient at removing lead in low concentrations (Ponnaiah et al., 2024).

---

### **5. Electrocoagulation**
Electrocoagulation involves the generation of metal ions through electrolytic dissolution of sacrificial anodes, leading to the coagulation and precipitation of lead ions.

- This technique performs well at removing lead from industrial wastewater and has even been enhanced using renewable energy sources like solar photovoltaic systems, making it a more sustainable option for developing regions (Hussin et al., 2017).

---

### **6. Biological Methods**
Microbial and phytoremediation techniques have emerged as viable alternatives for lead removal.

- **Lactic Acid Bacteria (LAB)**: Certain strains of LAB display a high affinity for lead, facilitating uptake through adsorption and bioaccumulation. These methods are especially effective for lead removal in low concentrations (Halttunen et al., 2007).
  
- **Plant-Based Remediation**:
  - Seedlings from peas, corn, and other plants demonstrate the ability to absorb lead ions through root systems when exposed to contaminated water. This method is particularly beneficial in soil-water systems or hybrid treatment protocols (SUN et al., 2014).

---

### **7. Advanced and Hybrid Methods**
To address the limitations of individual methods, researchers have developed combined or advanced techniques.

- **Nano-Engineered Sorbents and Membranes**: Hybrid techniques incorporating nanoparticles (e.g., Janus nanoparticles, graphene oxide, metal oxides) in existing methods, such as adsorption or membrane filtration, yield higher removal efficiencies (Khoee et al., 2024)(Pauli & Honciuc, 2022).
- **Geopolymer Adsorbents**: The use of industrial waste-derived geopolymers as adsorbents has been demonstrated to remove lead by combining ion exchange, precipitation, and surface adsorption mechanisms (Arokiasamy et al., 2022).
- **Capillary Systems**: Biopolymeric capillary tubes derived from natural sources (e.g., palm fronds, snail shells) have shown significant efficacy in lead removal, particularly in water with low lead concentrations (Onyechi & Igwegbe, 2025).

---

### **Key Factors Influencing Method Selection**
To select the most appropriate method for a specific application, factors such as the following should be considered:
- **Lead Concentration**: Techniques like adsorption or electrocoagulation are typically more effective for removing high concentrations of lead.
- **Water Composition**: The presence of competing ions or organic contaminants can affect selectivity and efficiency. Membrane filtration and ion exchange methods are more resistant to such interferences.
- **Cost and Scalability**: Natural or agricultural waste-based adsorbents are low-cost and environmentally sustainable, while advanced nanotechnology-based methods may involve higher initial costs.
- **Environmental Sustainability**: Materials derived from renewable sources or processes powered by renewable energy (e.g., solar-driven electrocoagulation) contribute to sustainability goals.

---

### Research Gaps and Future Directions
Continued research is needed to address challenges in large-scale implementation, waste management (e.g., disposal or recycling of spent adsorbents), and achieving high efficiency under varying water qualities. Developing low-cost, hybrid techniques and exploring novel materials (e.g., bio-composites or multifunctional membranes) will play a significant role in advancing lead-removal technologies.

ARSENIC

Arsenic removal from water is a critical process due to its significant health hazards, including carcinogenic and toxic effects. Numerous treatment methods have been developed to address this need, with varying degrees of efficiency, cost-effectiveness, and applicability depending on water quality, arsenic concentration, and geographic conditions. Below, a comprehensive review of proven arsenic removal methods is outlined:

### **1. Adsorption**
Adsorption is widely regarded as one of the most effective and flexible methods for arsenic removal, owing to its simplicity, efficiency, and the availability of diverse adsorbents.

- **Iron-based Adsorbents:** Iron oxide-coated materials or biochar impregnated with iron (e.g., Fe3O4 nanoparticles) have high adsorption capacities for arsenic. These materials can remove both As(III) and As(V) effectively, with the added advantage of magnetic properties for simplified separation after treatment (Indurkar et al., 2023)(Chen et al., 2021).
- **Biochar and Waste-based Adsorbents:** Materials such as biochar derived from agricultural waste or Pongamia pinnata hulls exhibit sustainability, low cost, and high efficiency in arsenic removal (Indurkar et al., 2023)(Chen et al., 2021).
- **Advanced Materials:** Nanomaterials such as ferrihydrite-graphene oxide foams (Shanmugaraj et al., 2023) and yttrium-doped Fe3O4 (Dojčinović et al., 2019) offer enhanced adsorption through unique surface properties, although their large-scale application is limited by cost and production complexity.

**Advantages:** 
- High efficiency even at low arsenic concentrations.
- Versatility in treating a range of water chemistries.

**Limitations:** 
- Potential fouling of adsorbents.
- Disposal of arsenic-saturated adsorbents poses an environmental concern.

---

### **2. Coagulation and Filtration**
This traditional method involves the use of coagulants like aluminum sulfate (alum) or ferric chloride to neutralize arsenic-containing particles, followed by filtration.

- **Oxidation-Coagulation at Optimized pH (OCOP):** A cost-effective variant that combines oxidation and coagulation to remove arsenic. Widely used in regions like India, KMnO₄ is employed as an oxidant to convert As(III) to the more easily removable As(V) (Bora & Dutta, 2022)(Bora et al., 2017).
- **Simultaneous Metal Removal:** Techniques that target co-contaminants such as iron and manganese, together with arsenic, optimize treatment efficiency and reduce costs (Bora et al., 2017).

**Advantages:** 
- Effective for large-scale treatment.
- Commonly available chemicals.

**Limitations:**
- Sludge generation.
- May require secondary treatment for sludge disposal.

---

### **3. Oxidation and Precipitation**
Arsenic is often found in its trivalent form (As(III)), which is more toxic and harder to remove than its pentavalent counterpart (As(V)). Oxidation processes convert As(III) to As(V), which can then be easily separated.

- **Conventional Oxidants:** Chlorine, ozone, hydrogen peroxide, or potassium permanganate are frequently used to achieve effective oxidation (Bora & Dutta, 2022).
- **Advanced Oxidation Processes (AOPs):** UV-assisted methods with agents like Fe(II)/UV/H₂O₂ or photo-oxidation using TiO₂ demonstrate enhanced arsenic oxidation and subsequent removal capabilities (Xing et al., 2019)(Guan et al., 2012).

**Advantages:**
- Facilitates removal of arsenic in conjunction with other contaminants.
- Suitable for high arsenic concentrations in groundwater.

**Limitations:**
- Infrastructure requirements for advanced processes.
- High operational costs for AOPs.

---

### **4. Membrane Filtration**
Membrane technologies such as **nanofiltration (NF)** and **reverse osmosis (RO)** are highly effective in removing dissolved arsenic.

- **RO Systems:** Utilize semi-permeable membranes to separate arsenic and other contaminants from water. While RO is highly effective, it is energy-intensive and produces reject water.
- **Electrospun Membranes:** PVDF membranes embedded with graphene oxide provide high arsenic removal (below detection limits) through enhanced adsorption and filtration mechanisms (Leaper et al., 2021).
- **Nanofiltration:** Effective at targeting specific arsenic species while consuming less energy compared to RO (Rajendran et al., 2020).

**Advantages:**
- High removal efficiency for a broad spectrum of contaminants.
- Can handle a wide range of water quality parameters.

**Limitations:**
- High initial and operational costs.
- Requires regular membrane cleaning and disposal of brine.

---

### **5. Ion Exchange**
Ion exchange resins with affinity for arsenic species are effective for targeted removal, particularly in water with high ion variability.

- **Cellulose-Based Anion Exchangers:** Custom anion exchangers with functionalities like tertiary amines selectively adsorb arsenic(V) ions, providing excellent performance in even complex water chemistries (Anirudhan & Jalajamony, 2010).
- **Hybrid Ion Exchange Resins:** These have been modified with iron oxides or other materials to enhance arsenic binding while lowering operational costs (Rivas, 2016).

**Advantages:**
- High selectivity for specific arsenic species (especially As(V)).
- Long operational lifespan.

**Limitations:**
- Requires regeneration of the resin bed, which could produce chemical waste.
- Less effective for polyvalent ion-rich waters.

---

### **6. Biological Methods**
Harnessing microorganisms and bioengineered systems for arsenic removal shows promise due to its eco-friendliness and versatility.

- **Fungal Bioremediation:** Aspergillus flavus CR500 can tolerate up to 800 mg/L Cr(V) while also effectively mitigating arsenate and arsenite through metabolic processes (Kumar & Dwivedi, 2019).
- **Bioaugmentation with Microorganisms:** Techniques that use bacteria such as Microbacterium esteraromaticum combined with granules can achieve >60% arsenic degradation in soil and water (Yadav et al., 2020).

**Advantages:**
- Environmentally benign and cost-effective.
- Potential for resource recovery.

**Limitations:**
- Slower kinetics compared to physical or chemical methods.
- Sensitivity to environmental conditions (e.g., temperature, pH).

---

### **7. Emerging Technologies**
New technologies, including **molecularly imprinted nanoparticles** and **magnetic biochars**, offer selective and efficient arsenic removal with significant potential for scale-up.

- **As(V)-Imprinted Polymers:** These nanoparticles demonstrated selective arsenic adsorption due to molecular imprinting, showcasing high removal efficiencies in controlled laboratory conditions (Karakoç et al., 2024).
- **Microwave-Assisted Bionanomaterials:** Materials treated with microwave energy display enhanced surface areas and adsorption properties (Jaishree et al., 2024).

**Advantages:**
- Ability to achieve ultra-low arsenic levels in water.
- High selectivity and reusability.

**Limitations:**
- High costs and limited field application.

---

### **Considerations for Selecting a Method**
The optimal arsenic removal technique depends on factors such as:
- **[Arsenic Speciation](@brm/pedia/kw:191241):** As(III) versus As(V).
- **Water Composition:** Presence of competing ions and co-contaminants.
- **Scale of Application:** Household, community, or industrial scale.
- **Economic and Geographic Factors:** Cost constraints, availability of resources, and local water quality conditions.

For large-scale and economically viable deployment, **coagulation coupled with filtration**, or adsorptive techniques using iron-based adsorbents, are most commonly recommended. Advanced technologies like nanomaterials and AOPs are better suited for niche applications requiring ultra-purified water or high arsenic concentrations.

**Future Outlook:**
Research into cost-effective, environmentally sustainable, and scalable solutions remains critical, especially for heavily impacted regions like Bangladesh and parts of India where arsenic contamination poses a severe public health crisis.

MERCURY

Mercury is a highly toxic heavy metal, and its removal from water is essential to protect human health and the environment. There are various methods to achieve mercury removal, ranging from traditional chemical approaches to advanced materials and biological processes. The choice of strategy depends on several factors, including mercury concentration, the form of mercury present (inorganic/organic/elemental), and the specific application (e.g., industrial wastewater, drinking water, or natural water systems). Below is a detailed analysis of effective methods for mercury removal:

---

### **1. Adsorption-Based Methods**
Adsorption is a widely applied method due to its simplicity, cost-effectiveness, and high efficiency. Various adsorbent materials have been developed for mercury removal:

- **Activated Carbon and Biochar**: 
  Activated carbon and biochar are commonly used adsorbents due to their large surface area and high porosity. Biochar derived from agricultural waste materials has shown promising results in mercury removal and is environmentally sustainable (Jagadeesh & Sundaram, 2023)(Bayar et al., 2024).
  
- **Metal Oxide-Based Adsorbents**: 
  Adsorbents like iron or manganese oxides have demonstrated high affinity for mercury ions, enabling efficient capture. For example, manganese-ferric (hydr)oxides effectively remove trace mercury from complex water matrices (Lu et al., 2014)(Raheem et al., 2024).

- **MXenes and Advanced Nanomaterials**: 
  MXenes, such as Ti3C2Tx, modified with carboxyl groups, show excellent adsorption capacity due to high surface area and chemical functionality. These materials achieve rapid mercury removal with minimal by-products (Isfahani et al., 2022).

- **Metal-Organic Frameworks (MOFs)**: 
  MOFs, such as sulfur-modified UiO-66, are highly effective adsorbents with a large surface area and tunable functional groups. They offer high adsorption efficiencies and rapid removal kinetics for mercury ions (Gao et al., 2021)(Georgin et al., 2024).

- **Thioether-Crown Polymers**: 
  Porous materials enriched with thioether-crown compounds have been shown to provide exceptionally high mercury uptake, with adsorption capacities exceeding 1,600 mg/g (Shetty et al., 2019).

**Advantages**: High efficiency, effective at low mercury concentrations, and relatively simple setup.  
**Limitations**: Adsorbent regeneration and disposal of adsorbed mercury can pose challenges.

---

### **2. Chemical Precipitation**
Chemical precipitation involves converting dissolved mercury into a solid phase that can be separated from water. It is particularly beneficial for treating high concentrations of mercury in industrial wastewater.

- **Sulfide Precipitation**: 
  Mercury ions react with sulfide ions to form highly insoluble mercuric sulfide (HgS), which can be removed through sedimentation or filtration (Ahmad et al., 2024)(Clercq, 2012).

- **Hydroxide Precipitation**: 
  Sodium hydroxide or lime can be used to precipitate mercury as insoluble hydroxide salts under controlled pH conditions (Mandal & Esa, 2017).

**Advantages**: Effective for high concentrations of mercury; simple scaling for industrial applications.  
**Limitations**: Generates sludge, which requires safe handling and disposal.

---

### **3. Membrane Technologies**
Membranes provide a physical barrier for separating mercury from water, utilizing selective filtration mechanisms.

- **Reverse Osmosis and Nanofiltration**: 
  These methods rely on semi-permeable membranes to effectively remove mercury along with other dissolved ions. Nanofiltration offers lower energy consumption while achieving a high removal rate for divalent mercury (Ajiboye et al., 2020).

- **Hybrid Membrane Adsorption Systems**: 
  Systems that integrate adsorbents, such as metal-organic frameworks and porous materials, within membranes improve mercury removal capacity and extend the operational life of membranes (Gao et al., 2021)(Hutchison et al., 2008).

**Advantages**: High removal efficiency and ability to treat a range of contaminants.  
**Limitations**: High operational costs and the need for periodic membrane maintenance.

---

### **4. Ion Exchange Methods**
Ion exchange resins are designed to selectively remove mercury ions from water:

- **Mineral-Based Ion Exchange Resins**: 
  Resins modified with sulfur or amine functional groups show a high affinity for mercury ions, enabling selective and efficient removal (Clercq, 2012)(Ajiboye et al., 2020).

- **Hybrid Resins**: 
  Ion exchange resins doped with nanoparticles or metals such as iron oxides enhance mercury binding and provide better adsorption rates (Sharma et al., 2014).

**Advantages**: High selectivity for mercury and long operational life.  
**Limitations**: Resin regeneration requires chemical treatment, leading to waste generation.

---

### **5. Biological Treatment**
Biological methods leverage the natural ability of microorganisms and plants to absorb and concentrate mercury:

- **Phytoremediation**: 
  Plants like *Salvinia natans* are used to remove mercury from water through rhizofiltration and bioaccumulation. This is a cost-effective and sustainable method for low-concentration mercury removal (Sitarska et al., 2023).

- **Microbial Remediation**: 
  Certain bacteria such as *Microbacterium esteraromaticum* can metabolize mercury compounds, converting toxic Hg(II) into volatile Hg(0) or sequestering it within biomass (Yadav et al., 2020).

**Advantages**: Environmentally friendly, suitable for low-cost remediation.  
**Limitations**: Slow process and sensitivity to environmental conditions.

---

### **6. Advanced and Emerging Technologies**
Recent advances in mercury removal strategies have focused on increasing efficiency and selectivity:

- **Electrocoagulation**: 
  This method uses electrical currents to destabilize mercury ions and precipitate them. It has been optimized for simultaneous removal of mercury and other co-contaminants like nickel and lead (Vasudevan et al., 2012).

- **Magnetic Nanoparticles**: 
  Iron oxide nanoparticles modified with functional ligands, such as 2-mercaptobenzothiazole, are fast, reusable, and safe for mercury removal in trace amounts (Parham et al., 2012).

- **Cryogels and Advanced Gels**: 
  Cryogels and polymeric hydrogels with functionalized surfaces are gaining attention for their rapid mercury adsorption, even under environmentally relevant conditions (Baimenov et al., 2020).

**Advantages**: Superior removal efficiency; potential for regeneration and repeated use.  
**Limitations**: High cost and limited commercial availability.

---

### **Selecting the Optimal Method**
The choice of method depends on application specifics, including:
- **Mercury Form**: Elemental mercury, ionic Hg(II), or organomercury compounds.
- **Scale**: Small-scale applications (e.g., drinking water systems) benefit from biochars or ion exchange, while large-scale systems (e.g., industrial discharge treatment) often incorporate precipitation or membrane technologies.
- **Economic and Environmental Constraints**: Cost-effective options like phytoremediation or biochars are preferable in resource-limited settings.

---

### **Future Directions**
Innovative technologies, such as nanomaterial-based adsorbents, hybrid membrane systems, and bioengineered solutions, hold great promise. Research should focus on cost reduction, scalability, and minimizing secondary waste generation to achieve sustainable mercury remediation. Rapid field validation of emerging materials like MXenes, MOFs, and biologically enhanced systems will shape their practical adoption.

CADMIUM

Cadmium (Cd) is a toxic heavy metal that poses significant health and environmental risks. The removal of cadmium from water is essential for mitigating its harmful effects. Various treatment technologies, ranging from adsorption and chemical precipitation to advanced and biological approaches, have been developed for cadmium remediation. Below is a detailed analysis of effective methods for cadmium removal from water, focusing on their mechanisms, advantages, and limitations.

---

### 1. **Adsorption-Based Methods**

Adsorption is one of the most effective strategies for cadmium removal due to its simplicity, scalability, and efficiency. The process involves binding cadmium ions onto the surface of adsorbent materials.

- **[Activated Carbon](@brm/pedia/kw:268402) and Biochar**:
  - Biochar derived from agricultural by-products (e.g., peanut shells, corn cobs) has proven highly effective. For instance, biochar activated at specific temperatures can achieve optimal cadmium adsorption due to its functional surface groups and large porosity (Puglla et al., 2020)(Anakhu et al., 2024).
  - Advantages: Cost-effective, sustainable, and applicable for low-concentration removal.
  - Limitations: Disposal of saturated adsorbents may pose environmental concerns.

- **Metal-Oxide Adsorbents**:
  - Iron oxides, manganese oxides, and modified biochars enriched with iron or manganese oxides have strong binding affinities for cadmium. These adsorbents show high availability for aquatic system applications (Anakhu et al., 2024)(Chwastowski & Staroń, 2024).
  - Advantages: High adsorption capacity and enhanced adsorption kinetics.
  - Limitations: Potential for reduced performance in the presence of competing ions.

- **Innovative Nanomaterials**:
  - Metal-organic frameworks (MOFs), MXenes, and graphene oxide composites exhibit exceptional cadmium removal capacities.
  - MOFs functionalized with sulfur compounds, for example, demonstrate highly specific cadmium affinities due to cadmium-sulfur bonding (Zheng et al., 2008)(Chwastowski & Staroń, 2024).
  - Advantages: Superior adsorption efficiency and selectivity, particularly for trace-level contamination.
  - Limitations: High production costs limit widespread use.

---

### 2. **Chemical Precipitation**

Chemical precipitation works by adding reagents to form insoluble cadmium compounds, which can then be removed via filtration or sedimentation.

- **Sulfide Precipitation**:
  - Treating water with substances like sodium sulfide (Na2S) precipitates cadmium as cadmium sulfide (CdS), an insoluble compound (Zheng et al., 2008).
  - Advantages: Effective at treating high concentrations of cadmium.
  - Limitations: Generates sludge that requires safe disposal.

- **Hydroxide Precipitation**:
  - Cadmium hydroxide (Cd(OH)2) can be precipitated by adjusting the pH with lime or sodium hydroxide.
  - Advantages: Simple, scalable, and relatively low-cost.
  - Limitations: Limited efficacy at low cadmium concentrations and in high ionic strength water.

---

### 3. **Membrane Filtration Technologies**

Membrane processes provide an advanced solution for cadmium removal. They involve the physical separation of cadmium ions using semi-permeable membranes:

- **Reverse Osmosis (RO)**:
  - Efficient in removing cadmium ions alongside other dissolved contaminants. RO achieves complete cadmium removal at the molecular scale.
  - Advantages: High efficiency and applicability in drinking water purification.
  - Limitations: High energy cost and fouling issues.

- **Nanofiltration (NF)**:
  - NF membranes, with selective pore sizes, can effectively separate Cd2+ ions. They operate at lower pressures, making them more energy-efficient than RO.
  - Advantages: Cost-effective for medium-scale operations.
  - Limitations: Performance can vary based on water quality and competing ions.

- **Hybrid Membrane-Adsorption Systems**:
  - Combinations of membrane techniques with materials such as MOFs or biochar enhance cadmium removal capacity while prolonging membrane lifespan.

---

### 4. **Ion Exchange**

Ion exchange involves the replacement of cadmium ions with non-toxic ions on a resin matrix.

- **Resin Types**:
  - Resins functionalized with chelating groups, such as thiol (-SH) or amine groups, selectively bind cadmium.
  - Hybrid ion exchange resins doped with nanoparticles or iron oxides improve ion removal efficiency (Chwastowski & Staroń, 2024).
  - Advantages: Highly selective and reusable.
  - Limitations: Regeneration of resins requires chemical treatment, which may produce waste streams.

---

### 5. **Electrochemical Methods**

Electrochemical techniques use electrical energy to reduce cadmium ions or induce localized precipitation.

- **Electrocoagulation**:
  - Aluminum or iron electrodes are used to generate coagulants in situ, which bind cadmium ions and remove them as precipitates.
  - Advantages: Effective for both high and low cadmium concentrations.
  - Limitations: Requires electrical energy and generates sludge.

- **Electrodialysis**:
  - Ion-selective membranes are used to separate cadmium ions under the influence of an electrical field.
  - Advantages: Minimal chemical additive requirements.
  - Limitations: High operational and maintenance costs.

---

### 6. **Biological Methods**

Biological removal methods leverage the metabolic capabilities of microorganisms and plants to absorb or transform cadmium ions.

- **[Phytoremediation](@brm/pedia/kw:151633)**:
  - Aquatic plants such as Salvinia natans and Eichhornia crassipes can absorb cadmium through bioaccumulation.
  - Advantages: Eco-friendly, cost-effective, and sustainable.
  - Limitations: Limited capacity for severe contamination.

- **Microbial Remediation**:
  - Certain bacteria, such as Pseudomonas and Bacillus species, can bioaccumulate cadmium or transform it into less toxic forms.
  - Advantages: Environmentally safe and suitable for low-concentration cadmium.
  - Limitations: Requires optimal environmental conditions for microbial activity.

---

### 7. **Emerging Technologies**

Recent advancements have introduced innovative materials and technologies to improve cadmium removal.

- **Magnetic Nanoparticles**:
  - Iron oxide nanoparticles functionalized with ligands or coatings efficiently adsorb cadmium ions and can be easily separated using magnetic fields (Anakhu et al., 2024).
  - Advantages: High adsorption capacity and reusability.
  - Limitations: High production costs.

- **Advanced Polymer Gels and Cryogels**:
  - Functionalized cryogels offer rapid cadmium adsorption due to their high porosity and active surface groups.
  - Advantages: Reusability and enhanced kinetics.
  - Limitations: Limited commercial scalability as of now.

---

### Considerations for Selection

The choice of cadmium removal method depends on several factors:
- **Cadmium Concentration**: Low-concentration waters may benefit from adsorption, while chemical precipitation suits high concentrations.
- **Water Chemistry**: Presence of other contaminants, pH, and ionic strength affect method selection.
- **Economic Feasibility**: Cost-effective methods such as biochar adsorption or ion exchange are preferred in resource-constrained regions.

### Future Directions

Research into hybrid methods combining chemical, biological, and adsorption technologies is critical to enhancing cadmium removal efficiency while minimizing waste. Sustainable solutions, like biochar and phytoremediation, remain key for developing eco-friendly water treatment systems, particularly in low-income regions.

### **1. Adsorption**
Adsorption is one of the most studied and widely applied techniques for nickel removal due to its cost-effectiveness, operational simplicity, and reusability of materials.

- **Activated Carbon and Biochar**: Activated carbons synthesized from agricultural waste or biochar provide high surface area and functional groups for nickel binding. Functionalized activated carbons have shown over **99% nickel removal efficiency under optimal pH 8.0 and specific conditions** (Siddiqui et al., 2020).
- **Nanomaterials**: Materials such as carbon nanotubes, graphene oxide (GO), and reduced graphene oxide (rGO) exhibit high adsorption capacity for nickel ions due to their exceptional surface area, tunable functionalities, and ease of chemical modification. GO-based composites have been identified as excellent adsorbents for heavy metal sequestration (Kumar et al., 2024).
- **Natural Adsorbents**: Agricultural by-products like fruit peels (biosorbents), biosorbent derivatives like chitosan, and lignocellulosic materials are eco-friendly alternatives. For example, modified cassava tuber peels effectively bind nickel ions through functional groups such as carboxyl and hydroxyls (Alongamo et al., 2021).
- **Synthetic Polymers**: Chitosan beads cross-linked with glutaraldehyde enhance nickel adsorption due to the chelation capabilities of amine groups (Ramli et al., 2025).

These methods operate predominantly on optimal pH values (often slightly acidic to neutral) and allow nickel’s selective adsorption, minimizing co-contaminant effects.

### **2. Ion Exchange**
Ion exchange is a highly effective method for treating water with low to moderate nickel concentrations.
- Resins such as Dowex HCR S/S, a strong acid cation-exchange resin, achieve above **98% ion removal efficiency** under appropriate conditions of pH and resin dosage (Alyüz & Veli, 2009).
- This method is particularly favorable for continuous flow industrial applications, offering scalability and selectivity for divalent ions such as Ni(II). Incorporating electricity-assisted ion exchange further enhances the efficiency of resin regeneration and nickel extraction (Ismail et al., 2013).

### **3. Membrane Filtration**
Membrane technologies hold remarkable advantages over conventional methods owing to their ability to eliminate even trace amounts of nickel effectively.
- **Reverse Osmosis (RO)**: RO membranes reject dissolved ions, including nickel, by relying on semi-permeable membranes and applied pressure. RO systems can remove up to **98%-99% of heavy metals**, making them suitable for industries requiring high-purity water (Jarrar et al., 2024).
- **Nanofiltration (NF)**: NF membranes offer a balance between cost and performance, selectively removing divalent cations like Ni(II) while allowing monovalent salts and smaller molecules to pass through. NF membranes are less energy-intensive compared to RO while maintaining similar heavy metal removal efficiencies (Zhang et al., 2021).
- **Electro-Membrane Processes**: Supported liquid membranes and electrodialysis (ED) provide excellent ion separation with reduced environmental footprint by integrating ion transport with electric fields. These methods also enable ion recovery, promoting circular economy potentials (Sulaiman et al., 2021).

### **4. Precipitation-Based Techniques**
Chemical precipitation is a widely employed method in wastewater treatment plants, particularly for high-concentration contaminants.
- **Hydroxide Precipitation**: Nickel hydroxide (Ni(OH)_2) is insoluble at pH 8-10 and can be precipitated efficiently by adjusting the water’s pH. The sludge generated can be treated further for disposal or material recovery (Tytkowska-Owerko et al., 2025).
- **Sulphide Precipitation**: Using sulfide compounds, nickel forms highly stable precipitates at broader pH ranges, reducing solubility concerns even under acidic conditions. This approach is especially applicable in treating industrial effluents with complex compositions (Kumar et al., 2017).

### **5. Electrochemical Processes**
Electrochemical treatments, such as electrocoagulation, are viable for simultaneous removal and recovery of nickel and other heavy metals.
- Electrocoagulation involves sacrificial electrodes (e.g., iron or aluminum) that release coagulants upon electric current application. Nickel ions are removed via complex adsorption and co-precipitation. Studies have demonstrated high removal efficiency under controlled operational and environmental conditions (Heffron et al., 2016).
- Additionally, electrochemical floatation shows promise in separating heavy metals from water through electrode-stabilized bubbles that carry contaminants to the surface for removal (Yun et al., 2024).

### **6. Bioremediation**
The use of bio-based materials, including bacteria, fungi, algae, and their derivatives, offers sustainable nickel removal options:
- **Microalgae** like *Chlorella vulgaris* and *Scenedesmus* species effectively adsorb and accumulate nickel ions under specific operating conditions (Isa & Sabri, 2025).

- **Hybrid Nanomaterials**: Composites combining conventional adsorbents with nanomaterials (e.g., magnetic graphene oxide, functional cryogels) amplify adsorption selectivity, binding capacity, and recovery feasibility (Evli et al., 2020).

Moreover, integrated resource recovery frameworks, combining the recovery of nickel alongside other valuable metals or by-products, will align nickel removal technologies with circular economy aspirations.

For site-specific solutions, preliminary batch or pilot-scale studies are recommended to assess optimal methods in terms of removal efficiency, resource consumption, and secondary waste management.


Classifying actions related to nickel removal and treatment into the categories of **Immediate Actions Required**, **Long-term Monitoring**, and **Positive Indicators** demands a structured approach based on the environmental impact, treatment efficiency, and sustainability of methods. Below is a breakdown of how these elements align with the removal and management of nickel in water:

---

### **Immediate Actions Required**
These actions involve urgent or short-term interventions to mitigate nickel's immediate impact on water systems and human or environmental health. They often address contamination sources or treat water with high nickel concentrations.

1. **Emergency Contamination Containment:**
   - Identify and isolate sources of nickel contamination, such as industrial effluents or accidental spills.
   - Deploy physical barriers or diversion structures to prevent further spread into water bodies.

2. **Rapid Chemical Precipitation or Coagulation:**
   - Use chemical agents (e.g., sodium hydroxide) for quick neutralization and precipitation of nickel hydroxide (Ni(OH)₂) at pH values between 8 and 10 (Vakili et al., 2020)(Tytkowska-Owerko et al., 2025). These precipitates can then be removed through sedimentation or filtration.

3. **Activated Carbon or Biochar Deployment:**
   - Rapidly deploy adsorbents like activated carbon derived from agricultural by-products to immobilize nickel ions in contaminated areas. This method is particularly useful for point-source pollution, where quick reduction in nickel concentrations is needed (Alongamo et al., 2021)(Puglla et al., 2020).

4. **Water Supply Substitution:**
   - Temporarily suspend water supplies from affected sources and rely on alternative clean water sources to minimize human and industrial exposure to nickel.

5. **Regulatory Compliance and Immediate Testing:**
   - Increase the frequency of water testing to determine the concentration of nickel and ensure compliance with legal standards (e.g., EPA or WHO guidelines).

---

### **Long-term Monitoring**
Long-term strategies ensure the sustained management of nickel contamination through regular observation, evaluation of remediation methods, and prevention of future risks.

1. **Groundwater and Surface Water Monitoring Systems:**
   - Install and maintain sensors or sampling stations to monitor nickel levels continuously in high-risk areas.
   - Regularly update spatial mapping of contamination zones to assess any dispersion or reduction trends (Tytkowska-Owerko et al., 2025).

2. **Performance Analytics of Treatment Plants:**
   - Evaluate the performance and effectiveness of treatment methodologies, such as ion-exchange resins or electrocoagulation, to ensure long-term applicability and optimize operational parameters.

3. **Ecosystem Health Studies:**
   - Assess the accumulation of nickel in aquatic ecosystems, including its bioaccumulation in fish and plants, as well as potential ecotoxicological effects over time.

4. **Mobile Adsorbent Regeneration Tracking:**
   - For reusable adsorbents like glutaraldehyde-crosslinked chitosan beads or specific biochars, monitor the efficiency of regeneration processes to manage costs and ensure continued efficacy (Ramli et al., 2025)(Puglla et al., 2020).

5. **Source Prevention via Industrial Practices:**
   - Implement and supervise zero-discharge policies or effluent controls in industries that release nickel into water systems, such as metal plating or battery manufacturing facilities (Tang & Ju, 2023)(Xu et al., 2024).

6. **Public Awareness and Policy Feedback:**
   - Regularly update communities and policymakers on the state of nickel levels, remediation progress, and required policy adjustments.

---

### **Positive Indicators**
Positive indicators represent measurable outcomes demonstrating successful progress in nickel removal, water system recovery, and sustainability of implemented practices.

1. **Efficient Removal Efficiency:**
   - Achieving consistent nickel removal efficiencies above 95% using advanced adsorption, precipitation, or membrane filtration techniques (Alongamo et al., 2021)(Ramli et al., 2025).

2. **Decrease in Nickel Concentrations:**
   - Water samples showing sustained reductions in nickel concentrations to levels below permissible limits (e.g., <0.02 mg/L for drinking water as per EPA standards).

3. **Optimal Adsorbent Utilization:**
   - High adsorption capacity and minimal regeneration loss in materials like biochar, chitosan beads, or nanocomposites over multiple treatment cycles (Vakili et al., 2020)(Ramli et al., 2025).

4. **Stable Ecosystem Recovery:**
   - Observations of reduced toxicity in aquatic species, such as a decline in bioaccumulation of nickel in critical aquatic organisms and growth of natural vegetation in previously contaminated water areas.

5. **Waste Minimization and Recycling:**
   - Implementation of sustainable practices, such as the recovery of nickel from sludge generated during precipitation or electrochemical treatment, for reuse in industrial applications (Ogata et al., 2020).

6. **Improved Public Health Metrics:**
   - Reduction in reported cases of nickel-related health issues, including skin rashes, respiratory problems, or carcinogenic risks.

7. **Reduced Operational Costs:**
   - Demonstrated cost-effectiveness of treatment methods with noticeable savings in both operating and environmental remediation expenses.

8. **Collaboration and Innovation:**
   - Cross-industry and governmental collaborations yielding technological advancements like hybrid methods or novel eco-friendly adsorbent materials for long-term nickel management (Wang et al., 2024)(Shen et al., 2024).

---

By implementing such measures across these categories, stakeholders can efficiently manage the risks associated with nickel contamination while ensuring environmental sustainability, regulatory compliance, and public health protection.
